What I learned from the Social Network Analysis Class

Xinran Zhu
 2021-05-07  493 words   3 minutes 
Contents

Introduction

This semester, I took a course titled CI 8371 - Applied Social Network Analysis in Education taught by Prof. Bodong Chen. This course introduced a novel research methodology - Social Network Analysis (SNA), with topics including the techniques, application, and the ethics of SNA in education research. SNA is powerful to unveil the invisible yet important connections between communications, concepts and many other kinds of networks.

I am intrigued by the word “connection”. In my life, no matter when I was watching a movie, or brainstorming about a design work, the most amazing moment is me starting to “see” new inspirations running to my head and connecting to my system. These moments always bring new ideas that make me feel so refreshed.

“Individuals are embedded in thick webs of social relations and interactions” - Borgatti et al., 2009

Even more, SNA can capture the dynamics of a network. This is really powerful since interaction is never linear. Each piece of interaction is interveined with other pieces.

This course has also triggered my interest in the field of information processing x learning science. With SNA’s affordance of visualizing connections, structures, and dynamics, it would be possible to track the knowledge flow or the information flow in support of understanding how humans learn with technologies. Why do we want to track that flow? As our brain has limited capacity in terms of processing information, especially in the working memory where we receive all of the new segments of information, we need to make notes all the time. But how many times do you take notes and never use it again (I always took thousands of pictures during a trip but never opened them again)? I think it is because, at least for me: 1. we tend to forget about the notes, and 2. we don’t know how and when to use these notes. It is important to make connections (the process of connecting is also a process of reinforcing the memory)! Therefore, if SNA techniques can help us make connections, we can further advance learning by using it as a “second brain” in support of connecting new information and previous obtained knowledge.

Course Project

The project I did in this course is a research proposal titled “Signature Analysis of Learners’ Participation in Collaborative Social Annotation Activities”. Rooted in the Computer Supported Collaborative Learning (CSCL) literature, this project explored the socio-cognitive participatory roles that are emerging from social annotation activities. SNA was used with a combination of content analysis, cluster analysis and statistical modeling.
